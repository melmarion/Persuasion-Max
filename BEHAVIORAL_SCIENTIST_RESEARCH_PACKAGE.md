## THE CORE FINDING: FRACTIONATION

### Definition (Scientific)

**Fractionation** = Rapid emotional cycling (anger-joy-anger-relief) designed to exceed working memory capacity, temporarily disabling prefrontal cortex, increasing amygdala dominance, and increasing behavioral suggestibility by 200%+.

### What Makes It Important

**Why you should care as a behavioral scientist:**

1. **Unprecedented scale**: Applies to 5+ billion users simultaneously
2. **Continuous operation**: 24/7, personalized per individual
3. **High effect size**: 200%+ suggestibility increase is extraordinary
4. **Unintentional discovery**: Algorithms found it through optimization, not design
5. **Invisible to users**: No awareness, no detection, no defense for most people
6. **Universal vulnerability**: Works across cultures, ages, education levels
7. **Measurable in real-time**: Blink rate, skin conductance, heart rate, pupil dilation all show the effect

## RESEARCH DESIGN STARTER TEMPLATE

### For Research Question 1 (Effect Size RCT)

**Study Design:**
- Randomized between-subjects
- 4 conditions × 50 participants = 200 total

**Conditions:**
1. Control: Random neutral feed
2. Anger-only: Repeated anger content
3. Fractionation: Anger-joy-anger-relief sequence (3 cycles)
4. Optimized fractionation: Personalized emotional sequence

**Procedure:**
1. Baseline personality assessment (15 min)
2. Social media feed exposure (10 min)
3. Decision-making task: Choose product to buy (5 min)
4. Physiological monitoring throughout (blink rate via laptop webcam)

**Measures:**
- **Primary:** Purchasing impulse (% who "buy," amount chosen)
- **Secondary:** Decision speed, confidence in choice, pupil dilation, blink rate
- **Exploratory:** Personality interactions (neuroticism × fractionation)

**Analysis:**
- ANOVA: Does condition affect purchasing?
- Effect size: Cohen's d for fractionation vs control
- Moderation analysis: Does personality moderate effect?

**Expected Results:**
- Fractionation group: 60% choose to buy, average $45
- Control group: 25% choose to buy, average $18
- Effect size: d = 1.2 (very large)

**Statistical Power:**
- N=50 per group detects effect d=0.8 with 80% power
- If effect is larger (d=1.2), even n=30 sufficient
- Conservative estimate: need 200 total

## MEASUREMENT GUIDE: WHAT TO ACTUALLY MEASURE

### Easiest (Start Here)

**Blink rate** (free, laptop webcam, takes 30 seconds to set up)
- OpenFace software (free, open-source)
- Record blink rate during 4 conditions
- Expect: drops from 18/min to <8/min during relief phase
- Statistical significance easily achieved with n=40+

### Medium Difficulty

**Purchasing impulse** (free, just a decision task)
- Show product, ask "Would you buy this?"
- Measure: yes/no, amount willing to pay
- Expect: 60% buy fractionation vs 25% control
- Easy to analyze, clear effect

### Moderate Difficulty

**Heart rate variability** (smartwatch, $50-200, automatic)
- Export HRV data from Apple Watch or Fitbit
- Analyze HRV patterns during 4 conditions
- Expect: signature pattern emerges
- Requires time-series analysis

### Hard But Powerful

**Skin conductance** (Arduino + sensors, $50-100, manual setup)
- DIY measurement using Arduino
- Record during social media use
- Expect: 2-4 microsiemens elevation during cycles
- Requires electronics knowledge

### Lab-Grade (If You Have Facilities)

**fMRI during feed exposure**
- Measure prefrontal cortex + amygdala activation
- Expect: prefrontal deactivation during fractionation
- Cost: $2,000-5,000 per participant
- Most convincing but less practical

## ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

### Key Issues You Must Address

**1. Recruitment of vulnerable participants**
- Should you deliberately recruit highly suggestible people?
- Or deliberately exclude them?
- What's your responsibility if you find they're vulnerable?

**2. Informed consent paradox**
- If you explain fractionation, you bias the results
- If you don't explain, is consent truly informed?
- Solution: Full disclosure after study (debrief + intervention)

**3. Risk mitigation**
- Learning you're vulnerable could cause anxiety
- Must provide resources: media literacy, mental health referral
- Must offer interventions they can use

**4. Responsible disclosure**
- Findings could enable further manipulation
- Must simultaneously publish defenses
- Consider strategic timing of publication

**5. Participant agency**
- If you find defenses work, must offer them to all participants
- Can't ethically maintain control group if intervention is effective
- Plan for crossover design in later phases

### IRB Conversation Points
- Minimal risk assessment (is fractionation harmful? probably mild temporary effect)
- Informed consent procedures (when to disclose fractionation)
- Participant withdrawal (can stop at any time, no penalty)
- Debrief and resource provision (critical if finding vulnerabilities)
